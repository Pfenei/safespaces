1. Abstract

The term “Safe Space” involves places for ostracised individuals where they are free of any kind of violence that could stop them from being themselves. It started in university campuses all over the western world and has evolved into other different areas such as workplaces. The most common safe spaces are dedicated to women, LGBTQ+ individuals, racialized people, and different religious backgrounds. The beginning of this movement always implied physical places where people could meet, but nowadays the discussion has moved to the internet world. With a society that is moving more and more towards a technological future and the upcoming generations joining the net sooner every time, the internet usage is something we all are or will be confronting sooner than later.

With this tool, having access to anything from the best to the worst content is something relatively easy to do, and that’s why the debate on whether to have spaces where younger and marginalized people can stay without receiving any harm. Some people think it’s the best way to go, offering security to everyone and mitigating the trolling and abusive behaviours; but for others, it may be something that stifles the freedom of speech and that should never be settled. This research is to investigate the story of the safe spaces, how they were created and what have they become, and explore different sides of opinions to know whether they are necessary or not.

2. What is a Safe Space?

A safe space is any place, physical or virtual, where people is free from judgement or criticism so they can feel safe, welcome, and supported. According to Tamara White’s Goldsmiths Student’s Safe Space Union Policy (2012), it’s “all about creating an open and welcoming environment within our Union so that all our members feel able to participate; Racism, homophobia, biphobia, sexism, transphobia, disablism or prejudice based on age, ethnicity, nationality, class, gender, gender presentation, language ability, immigration status or religious affiliation is unacceptable and will be challenged.”

The guidelines used by this group that are present in the previously mentioned document (Tamara White, Goldsmiths Students’ Union Student Assembly Policy 2012/13) are a very good example of what Safe Spaces should be built around:

Photo by Gabby K from" Pexels (taken October 3, 2011)

  • “Be aware of the connotations of your language. Many common expressions use discriminatory language so think before speaking. Be aware of the language you use in discussions and how you relate to others.”
  • “Try to speak slowly and clearly and use uncomplicated language.”
  • “Do not make assumptions about anyone, no matter what the topic is: gender, pronouns, sexual preference, abilities, ethnic identity, survivor status, or life experiences. Do not be derogatory to anyone about these things.”
  • “Be prepared to challenge hateful, discriminatory, or oppressive language. If you are challenged, do not become defensive, but listen, think, and learn.”
  • “Respect each other’s physical and emotional boundaries, always get explicit verbal consent before touching someone or crossing boundaries.”
  • "If a discussion becomes personal, we trust that participants will keep what is said inside the room and behave with kindness and consideration when responding.”
  • “Participants should be considerate of how much they are speaking to avoid dominating the conversation and avoid interrupting other people who are sharing their views. We encourage participants to listen to views which are different from their own but feel confident in expressing opposition in a non-confrontational way. Harassment, hostility, and aggression will not be tolerated in any form and if we feel that you have overstepped these boundaries, we will challenge this, and you may be asked to leave.”
  • “GSU recognises discrimination can occur wherever it is not consciously challenged. Upholding this policy is a collective responsibility and people should be confident in challenging any behaviour which contravenes this policy. We also, however, recognise that individuals should be responsible for their own behaviour, so should you feel that the policy has been breached and appropriate action was not taken at the time, speak to the organisers. All concerns will be taken seriously and dealt with in accordance with our comments and complaints procedures.”
3. History of Safe Spaces and some Nowadays Examples

The exact moment of the creation of the safe spaces is unknown, but Jennifer Gunn explained in her classroom resources article (Opening the Doors to Learning Through Safe Spaces in K-12 Schools) that some trace it back to the 1960s women’s movement, as a mean to create protective spaces for women against violence and provide a community to make changes.

One example of this kind of safe space in the present time would be the United Nations Entity for Gender Equality and the Empowerment of Women (also known as UN Women). In their Executive Summary about Sexual Harassment (March 2021), their key survey findings were that “71% of women of all ages in the UK have experienced some form of sexual harassment in a public space. This number rises to 86% among 18-24-year-olds and only 3% of 18-24-year-olds reported having not experienced any of the types of harassment listed”.

Women's Strike for Equality in New York around Fifth Avenue August 26, 1970, by RV1864 for Flickr (taken October 3, 2011)

The Northwestern University Gay Liberation Group attending the Anti Vietnam War Demonstration in Washington DC, by Skillful654321 for Wikipedia

Not long after the women’s movement settled the safe spaces culture, the term extended to the LGBTQ community. In the Vox article Safe spaces, explained by Emily Crockett (Aug 2016 ), she presents the movement with the words of Malcom Harris quoting the scholar and activist Moira Kenney’s book Mapping Gay L.A, where she introduces the origins in gay and lesbian bars in the mid-1960s:

“With anti-sodomy laws still in effect, a safe space meant somewhere you could be out and in good company — at least until the cops showed up. Gay bars were not ‘safe’ in the sense of being free from risk, nor were they ‘safe’ as in reserved. A safe place was where people could find practical resistance to political and social repression.”

A modern example of this branch of the movement would be the GLSEN, the Gay, Lesbian & Straight Education Network, created in 1990. They provide a Safe Space Kit Documentation (2016) for school, so the educators can set safe environments anywhere they need, and its guide contains four main sections:

  • “Know the Issues – Gives background information about the experiences of LGBT students and anti-LGBT bias.”
  • “Support – describes specific actions you can take to be an effective support to LGBT students.”
  • “Educate – discusses ways to teach students and inform school staff about combating anti-LGBT bias and behaviour.”
  • “Advocate – provides strategies you can use to promote change within your school.”

Since then, and with a huge increase in people’s awareness, a wide spectrum of safe spaces to satisfy specific purposes have been created. In an article for L.A Times named Campuses are breaking apart into ‘Safe Spaces’ (Jan 2017), Frank Furedi comments that “in 2016 and 2016, students of colour at many colleges called for segregated safe spaces”. There is no significative organization that focuses on the creation and distribution of safe spaces about the people of colour community yet, but there are some entrepreneurs working to push that agenda into their workplace. In an article for Vault written by Stephan Maldonado, he interviews Mikaela Kiner, author of the book Female Firebrands: Stories and Techniques to Ignite Change, Take Control, and Succeed in the Workplace. In the book, she works with thirteen successful women to learn about their barriers in their workplace, and combined with her years working for big companies HR departments, she launched a healthy, inclusive HR consulting service primarily focused on BIPOC women, but open to other scenarios.

Even if these are the three main branches people are focusing in creating safe spaces for, there are a plethora of groups and communities with safe spaces policies to ensure everyone’s security, for example:

  • The Bild Organization focuses on helping people with any kind of disabilities and provides resources and qualifications to ensure the expandability of these safe spaces.
  • The Bishop’s conference of England and Wales is launching safe spaces across the country for church-related victims of abuse and women among the religious communities.
  • UK Says No More is a campaign to help victims of domestic abuse and sexual violence and raise awareness on the issue.
4. Why is it good or bad to have safe spaces?

The response to safe spaces has been wide and is constantly changing upon the most recent events. It has proven to be a valuable resource that can turn into wrong ways when poorly executed. Here are some examples of good and bad opinions to present an overall view of the impact and situation of safe spaces.

Megan Yee explains in an article for Healthline (updated Jan 2019) about the importance of safe spaces for mental health that:

“when students ask for a safe space, we’re not trying to limit the flow of ideas on campus or to disengage from the community. Impeding free speech and censoring opinions that may not align with our own isn’t the objective. Instead, we’re seeking a tool to help us take care of our mental health so we can continue actively engaging in our classes, extracurriculars, and other areas of our lives.”

Jackie Hagan's Twitter Profile Picture

Another Example would be Carrie Aimes This is not a Safe Space acting show. (Disability Horizons, 2019) explains that:

“With an emphasis on class, mental health and disability, Hagan’s performances celebrate the weird and wonky lives of those excluded from the mainstream. Jackie Hagan describes herself as a queer, bipolar amputee, raised on a council estate. Her work seeks to challenge the ways in which current society relentlessly stereotypes working-class, disabled individuals”.

Among the negative criticism most notorious examples, John Ellison, as the dean of students of the University of Chicago, wrote a letter to all new students positioning the university against trigger-warnings and safe spaces’ censorship (2016). He wrote:

“Our commitment to academic freedom means that we do not support so-called trigger warnings, we do not cancel invited speakers because their topics might prove controversial and we do not condone the creation of intellectual safe spaces where individuals can retreat from ideas and perspectives at odds with their own.”

Another interesting response to safe spaces with a more positive ending is presented in Liz Stillwaggon article for Psychology Today (March 2017), In which she explains the dilemma she had to face when a student with social anxiety disorders emailed her about bailing a class debate she had to present. When she raised the petition to the counsellors to explore all the possible options, they advised her to allow the student to not take part of the debate class, since the pressure could affect very negatively to her progression or even feel tempted to drop the course. She didn’t expect this response, since all she wanted was to try and help the student overcome her fears but accepted the proposition. In her response email, she explains:

“I gave the student an "out," saying that if she absolutely could not bring herself to do it, she should just be “sick” that day, and her teammates could surmise about her absence whatever they wanted. But, I wrote, I strongly encourage you to do it. It’s awful the first time around, but it does get easier. Just let it be practice. Isn’t college all about practice and learning and challenging yourself? We’re a friendly group, and your teammates are counting on you, blah blah blah. Oh yeah, and it’s your midterm grade.”

And thanks to those encouragement words, she ended up going to her debate class and achieving full grade for it. This example demonstrates that, even with the vast number of positive features a safe space has to offer, it can turn into an isolated-from-reality pit that’s very hard to escape from.

5. Summary

Humans are sociable beings and we build communication and networks in mostly every action we do, even if it is with ourselves, and safe spaces are the perfect tool to help us understand each other better and have healthier relationships. It provides a background for ostracised people to present themselves how they are without fear of being judged or harassed. Combining this with methods and resources to improve someone’s conflict processing and resolution would develop superhumans for the future generations, because it’s not just a direct impact over the individual, but they way they live, work, relate to others, etc. Feeling better with yourself will make you happier and more productive in general, and that’s something everybody should aim for.

With that in present, there must be some guidelines along its policies to ensure everyone’s voice is being heard and that the freedom of one is not the prison of others. Limiting someone’s opinion wouldn’t allow both parts to grow in any means: Every opinion should be valid (that differs from right or wrong) as long the person is willing to listen for positive feedback and ways to improve in case they are taking the wrong approach for that situation.
